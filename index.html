Article

Please read the article below carefully.

When Daniel Kahneman, the Nobel Prize-winning economist, was asked whether humanity could solve the climate crisis, he didn't sugarcoat it. "Climate change is really the kind of threat that we have not evolved to cope with." "It's too distant. It's too complex."

Many psychologists agree. The problem, they say, comes down to how our brains are built. When it comes to climate change, our human brains are inclined toward denial, with nearly no part of our psyche helping us reach an understanding that encourages positive action.

We're built to care more about what's happening today than what might happen in twenty years. For most of human history, the dangers that mattered were right in front of us — a predator, a flood, a rival tribe. Our brains got good at reacting fast to threats we could see and feel. Harvard psychologist Daniel Gilbert calls the human brain a "get-out-of-the-way machine," built to dodge a baseball or run from a snake in a split second. Climate change doesn't work that way. It unfolds little by little over decades, too slow for us to notice. As experts have warned, the problem isn't that people don't care. It's that it's moving too slowly to trigger any sense of urgency.

From a biological perspective, our personal needs — a new outfit, an affordable flight, a full fridge — feel far more urgent to our brains than abstract global threats. In other words, struggling to act on climate change doesn't make us selfish — it makes us human. As Elke Weber, professor of psychology at Columbia University, put it: "In a way, it's unfair to expect people, homo sapiens, to do this kind of decision making, because we're not wired for that".

And that is the irony at the heart of the climate crisis. We are arguably the most intelligent species to have ever walked the earth, and yet, when faced with the greatest threat to our own survival, our brains simply look the other way. Not out of stupidity or cruelty, but because we were never built for this.
